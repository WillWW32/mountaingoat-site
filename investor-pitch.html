The Opportunity

This isn't just a basketball game.
It's Missoula's story.

Three kids grew up playing basketball together from the 4th grade through 7th grade in Missoula, Montana. Then they split to three rival high schools—Big Sky, Sentinel, and Hellgate—and became the best players in the state. Add in the most dominant female player Montana has ever produced, and you have a once-in-a-generation story.

Mountain Goats is a Hoop Dreams–style documentary capturing the Goat Game 2026—a packed-arena showdown at Adams Center where these childhood friends face off as rivals. Raw footage. No scripts. No narration. Just real kids chasing real dreams.

From the 4th grade on, Sia, Lincoln, and Zane were inseparable. Same gym. Same coaches. Same dream. By 7th grade, everyone knew these three would be special.

Then high school split them across Missoula's three biggest rivals. The kid who practiced with you every day is now the kid trying to end your season. The friend who fed you passes is now the opponent scouting your weaknesses.

Lincoln, the most acclaimed of the three, had the local D1 programs make 8–10 offers to California players and rescind his scholarship, hoping they could get him as a budget walk-on. He committed to a D2 that believed in him. Then he sat out the Goat Game—and responded by leading the #1 team in the state and becoming the #1 ranked player in Montana. Sia, the one nobody recruited, has to step up. And Zane, battling through injuries, refuses to let pain define his legacy.

"We thought we were making a basketball documentary. We were wrong. It's about friendship, loss, and what it takes to keep going when nobody's watching."

Making History

Perhaps Montana's greatest female player.

Avory

In a state where high school basketball is practically religion, Avory isn't just the best girl playing right now—she might be the best to ever come out of Montana. Period. Her intensity on the court is unmatched, and her story adds a dimension to this film that makes it more than a boys' rivalry documentary. She represents an entire generation of young female athletes in Montana who are rewriting what's possible.

Including Avory in the film broadens the audience dramatically. Her story resonates with families, female athletes, Title IX advocates, and anyone who believes the next generation of women's sports deserves a spotlight. For sponsors, her presence opens doors to brands and demographics that a boys-only documentary simply can't reach.
